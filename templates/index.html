What is Cervical Cancer?

Understanding the basics

Overview

Cervical cancer occurs in the cells of the cervix â€” the lower part of the uterus that connects to the vagina. Various strains of the human papillomavirus (HPV), a sexually transmitted infection, play a role in causing most cervical cancer.

When exposed to HPV, a woman's immune system typically prevents the virus from doing harm. However, in a small percentage of women, the virus survives for years, contributing to the process that causes some cervical cells to become cancer cells.

Did you know? Cervical cancer is one of the most preventable and treatable forms of cancer when detected early.

Risk Factors

What increases your risk of cervical cancer

HPV Infection

Human papillomavirus (HPV) infection is the most important risk factor for cervical cancer. HPV is a group of more than 150 related viruses, some of which can cause cancer.

About 14 HPV types are considered high-risk for cervical cancer. The most concerning are HPV 16 and 18, which cause about 70% of cervical cancers worldwide.

Smoking

Women who smoke are about twice as likely to develop cervical cancer as non-smokers. Tobacco by-products damage cervical cells' DNA and contribute to cancer development.

Smoking also makes the immune system less effective at fighting HPV infections.

Contraceptives

Long-term use of oral contraceptives (5+ years) may increase cervical cancer risk, though the risk decreases after stopping.

Intrauterine devices (IUDs) may actually lower cervical cancer risk, though more research is needed.

Prevention & Screening

How to protect yourself

HPV Vaccination

The HPV vaccine can prevent infection with the types of HPV most likely to cause cancer. Recommended for ages 9-45.

  • Nearly 100% effective against targeted HPV types
  • Best when given before sexual activity begins
  • Still important to get regular screenings

Regular Screening

Cervical cancer screening includes the Pap test and HPV test. Screening can find abnormal cells before they turn into cancer.

  • Pap test: Recommended every 3 years for women 21-65
  • HPV test: Can be done alone or with Pap test
  • Screening can prevent most cervical cancers
